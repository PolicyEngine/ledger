401(k) Savings and Participation Rates Rise

PSCA's 67th Annual Survey of 401(k) Plans also indicates employers are focused on implementation of SECURE Act provisions and cybersecurity.
Adjust My News Preferences

Though not yet back to 2021’s record highs, contribution rates made modest gains in 2023 as employers and participants contributed a little bit more to their plans after pulling back the year before, according to PSCA’s newly released 67th Annual Survey of Profit Sharing and 401(k) Plans, reports on the 2023 plan-year experience of 709 401(k) plans.


Participation and Savings
Nearly ninety percent of eligible employees had an account balance in 2023 (88.0 percent) and 86.9 percent made deferrals (up from 85.6 percent in 2022). Participants contributed an average of 7.8 percent of pay (up from 7.4 percent), and organizations contributed 4.9 percent of pay (up from 4.8 percent).

Distributions
While savings rates increased, plan loans decreased. Fewer participants borrowed against their plan assets in 2023, with lower average balances. At the same time, more participants took a hardship withdrawal in 2023

  • 5% of participants had a loan outstanding in 2023 (down from 18.6%)
  • The average loan amount was $10,628 average loan amount, down from $15,023 – total assets loaned accounted for 0.9% of plan assets (down from 1.4% the year before).
  • 2.1% of participants took a hardship withdrawal, up from 1.5% in 2022.

Plan Design Trends
Several design features we have been monitoring in recent years continued to gain traction. More plans are offering Roth, managed accounts, and using mobile technology to reach participants.   

  • Managed Accounts: Half of plans (50.1%) offer managed accounts to participants, up from 46.9% in 2022 and 43.6% in 2021.
  • Roth: Roth after-tax contributions are now available at 93 percent of plans, up from 89 percent in 2022. Twenty-one percent of participants made Roth contributions when given the opportunity.
  • Mobile Tech: Seventy percent of organizations use mobile technology to provide plan access, up from 63 percent in 2022.

Cybersecurity also continues to be a focus for plan sponsors – the use of a written cybersecurity policy increased to 31 percent of plans, up from 22 percent three years ago. Additionally, 72 percent use multifactor identification, up from 67 percent the year before.

ad space

SECURE 2.0 Provisions

Administratively, plan sponsors were focused on implementing mandatory provisions of SECURE 2.0 and deciding which optional provisions to implement. The optional provisions regarding distributions seem to be the most popular SECURE 2.0 provisions, with little uptake (so far) on most of the others.

  • 72% of organizations adopted the distributions for natural disasters.
  • More than half (52.3%) adopted the distribution in the event of a qualified birth or adoption (QBAD).
  • Nearly half (48.9%) adopted the distribution in the event of a terminal illness diagnosis provision.
  • Only two percent of plans are adding an employer match on student loan payments.
  • Less than one percent are adding a PLESA (emergency savings side-car account) and 29.3% are adopting the provision allowing a $1,000 emergency withdrawal.

While maintaining contributions in 2023, employers were focused on implementing design features and optional provisions of both SECURE Acts to best help their participants balance immediate financial needs with long term financial security. After dropping off in last year’s survey, retirement plans are bouncing back in the slow and steady fashion they tend to do during periods of economic volatility. The increase in plan savings and participation rates reflects the overall strength of the 401(k) system, particularly in the face of economic headwinds and uncertainty.

PSCA’s 67th Annual Survey is available for purchase here: https://www.psca.org/research/401k
